The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

The SDGs are:

  • A set of 17 goals for the world's future, through 2030
  • Negotiated over a two-year period at the United Nations
  • Agreed to by nearly all of the world's nations on 25 September 2015

Important Principles of the SDGs

üåç Universality

Goals apply to every nation and every sector. Cities, businesses, schools, organizations - all are challenged to act.

Why it matters: The challenges of poverty, climate change, and inequality require a universal response from all nations from the global north and south.

üîó Integration

Goals are all interconnected in a system, and real-world problems are complex and don't fit in one category only.

We cannot aim to achieve just one Goal. We must achieve them all (17 goals and 169 targets) since they depend on one another.

This requires systemic change across all sectors of society and economy.

Impacts of Humanitarian Situations

Direct Impacts

Examples include injury or death from the crisis itself.

Indirect Impacts

Examples include changes in living conditions, forced displacement, lack of legal protection, or decreased access to healthcare.

  1. Sanitation and water
  2. Food
  3. Shelter
  4. Social instability

‚ö†Ô∏è Addressing one without the other is insufficient.

Focus on health and peace: Lack of access to basic social services can lead to marginalization and instability.

  • Increased morbidity and mortality due to immediate effects of conflict, including war-related injuries, sexual violence, forced labour, exploitation and unlawful detention
  • Mass displacement intensifies poor health conditions and increases prevalence of communicable diseases such as malaria and respiratory infections
  • Exposure to war-related atrocities contributes to increased prevalence of psychological disorders, including PTSD, depression and anxiety
  • The great majority of humanitarian settings are in developing countries where health facilities, goods, and services are inadequate or essentially unavailable
  • Humanitarian crises can lead to destruction of health facilities and supplies, flight of health workers, and breakdown in services
  • Refugees/IDPs carry additional burdens in already resource-poor environments
  • They compete with local residents for limited supplies and assistance

Severity of Humanitarian Situations

The UN has three categories of emergencies:

Level 1: Localised Emergency

A localised emergency that can be managed with local resources.

Example: Boston marathon bombing

Level 2: Larger Scale Emergency

An emergency at a larger scale but can still be dealt with within the capacity of the agencies, government and other actors present in-country.

Example: La Palma volcano eruption

Level 3: International Response Required

The largest type of emergency that requires an international response, with the need for capacity and resources to be 'surged' into the country to help with the response.

Examples: Beirut blast, Syrian war, Tsunami

Humanitarian Settings

Two types of displaced populations:

  • Internally displaced
  • Refugees

In either case, they settle in:

  • Camps/informal settlements
  • Urban areas
  • Where displacement occurs within a country (internally displaced people), the International Organization for Migration (IOM) is often the lead agency in managing camps
  • When displacement occurs across borders (refugees), UNHCR has the global mandate for protection and will lead or co-lead camp management with the government of the receiving country
  • In refugee camps, you need to be aware of the rules and regulations for accessing and living on camp
  • Some camps are fortified aid compounds, with secure offices and residential complexes, alongside restrictive security and travel protocols

Challenges:

  • Increased inequality
  • Resource scarcity
  • Competition with the host community
  • Social conflict

Host community challenges:

  • Often underprivileged communities themselves
  • Refugees in urban settings blend in with the host community, making it difficult to collect and track information about them

Phases of Humanitarian Response

There are different phases in humanitarian situations, especially for slow onset and protracted crises:

Under Fire Phase
  • Getting exposed to atrocities
  • Being under direct shelling (war conflict)
  • Losing shelters and livelihoods
During Displacement

People witness challenges in:

  • Accessing shelter, food, and water
  • Accessing immediate attention to injuries
  • Accessing essential healthcare services
  • Accessing medications
Protracted Crises

The average conflict lasts ~17 years and leads to protracted crises which are devastating and have lifelong effects, particularly on the provision and sustainability of healthcare.

Challenges include:

  • Sustainability of livelihood: shelter, food, water, hygiene, essential healthcare services (injury and rehabilitation, psychological support, chronic care)
  • Sources of income: cash assistance, employment, debts
  • Repatriation/immigration

Response by Phase

Initial Phase: Access to life-saving healthcare is critical. Often local community addresses emerging issues (search and rescue, attending to injuries).

Transition Phase: Securing shelter, water and food. Treatment, prevention and palliative care.

Protracted Phase: Health promotion, prevention, treatment, rehabilitation and palliative care. Focus on other sectors (overcrowding, inadequate shelter, poor sanitation, insufficient water, reduced food security).

Why Do We Need to Respond?

Everyone has the right to timely and appropriate service

Grounded in the principles of the Humanitarian Charter:

  • Right to life with dignity
  • Right to protection and security
  • Right to receive humanitarian assistance

Guiding Principles: The HDP Nexus

Humanitarian ‚Äì Development ‚Äì Peace Nexus (Triple Nexus)

The HDP nexus connects short-term emergency help with long-term development and peacebuilding.

Instead of working separately:

  • Humanitarian workers provide emergency healthcare, food, and shelter
  • Development actors strengthen health systems, education, and livelihoods
  • Peacebuilders reduce conflict and promote trust and stability

Core Concept:

Health services should be equally, continuously and resiliently delivered.

Guiding Principles for Implementation

Interventions should apply:

  • Early recovery approaches at the onset of acute emergencies
  • Long-term vision during protracted emergencies
  • Seek integration with existing health services and transition of governance to local authorities
  • Address bottlenecks in health systems that can constrain humanitarian response

Implementation needs to be supported by:

  • Predictable and flexible humanitarian and development funding
  • Coordination and dialogue between all partners
  • Mapping of financing flows including non-traditional donor groups

In conflict-affected settings: Interventions should be designed so they do not exacerbate root causes of conflict and where possible contribute to peacebuilding.

Early Recovery Approach

Early recovery is an approach that addresses recovery needs that arise during the emergency, using humanitarian mechanisms aligned with development principles.

It enables people to use the benefits of humanitarian action to seize development opportunities, builds resilience, and establishes a sustainable process of recovery from crisis.

Actors in Humanitarian Response

The actors can be generally grouped into five categories:

  • UN agencies
  • Red Cross and Red Crescent Movement
  • Non-governmental organisations (NGOs) and civil society organisations (CSOs)
  • Governments
  • Non-traditional actors (everybody else!)

Crisis-affected populations are sometimes the target group for humanitarian assistance, but they are also the first responders to humanitarian crises.

This includes:

  • Local citizens
  • Municipal government workers
  • Community-based and faith-based organisation staff
  • Volunteers
  • Social entrepreneurs
  • And others

Ultimately governments are responsible for their citizens in the wake of an emergency.

  • Some governments have National Disaster Management Authorities, others have different government bodies that respond
  • In some cases, the UN will take on the role of leading a humanitarian response
  • Where the UN takes the lead role, coordination of humanitarian action is the mandate of the UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (UN OCHA)

Minimum Standards in Humanitarian Response

Standard 1.1: Health Service Delivery

People have access to integrated quality healthcare that is safe, effective and patient-centred.

Standard 1.2: Healthcare Workforce

People have access to healthcare workers with adequate skills at all levels of healthcare.

Standard 1.3: Essential Medicines

People have access to essential medicines and medical devices that are safe, effective and of assured quality.

Standard 1.4: Health Financing

People have access to free priority healthcare for the duration of the crisis.

Standard 1.5: Health Information

Healthcare is guided by evidence through the collection, analysis and use of relevant public health data.

Essential Healthcare: Trauma & Communicable Diseases

  • Provide care for trauma at all levels for all patients
    • Quickly establish safe referral systems between facilities
    • Establish mobile clinics or field hospitals if fixed structures are not accessible
  • Ensure healthcare workers have the skills and knowledge to address injuries
    • Include all levels from first responders to those providing definitive surgical and anaesthetics care
  • Establish or strengthen standardised protocols for triage and injury and trauma care
  • Ensure timely access to mental health services and psychosocial support
  • Establish or strengthen health information systems to include injury and trauma data

Objective: Prevent communicable diseases from the beginning, manage any cases, and ensure rapid and appropriate response if there is an outbreak.

Key Actions:

  • Determine the risk of communicable diseases (risk assessment)
  • Work with other sectors to develop general prevention measures and establish integrated health promotion programmes at community level
    • Address specific fears, rumours and common beliefs
    • Coordinate with hygiene promoters, community nutrition workers
  • Implement disease-specific prevention measures as needed

Example: COVID-19 & Beirut Blast responses highlight coordination with WASH and risk communication

Essential Healthcare: Child Health

Programmes should address the major causes of morbidity and mortality.

Globally these risks are:

  • Acute respiratory infections
  • Diarrhoea
  • Measles
  • Malaria
  • Malnutrition
  • Neonatal causes of morbidity and mortality
  • Determine whether there is a need for vaccinations, and the appropriate approach for the emergency
  • Conduct a mass measles vaccination campaign for children aged six months to 15 years, regardless of measles vaccination history, when estimated measles coverage is less than 90% or unknown
  • Screen children attending healthcare facilities or mobile clinics for vaccination status and administer any needed vaccinations

Screen children for their growth and nutritional status.

  • Refer all malnourished children to nutritional services
  • Provide facility-based treatment for children suffering from severe acute malnutrition with complications

Essential Healthcare: Sexual & Reproductive Health

Sexual and reproductive healthcare must respect cultural backgrounds and religious beliefs while meeting universally recognised international human rights standards.

‚ö†Ô∏è Emergencies elevate risks of sexual violence, including exploitation and abuse. All actors should work together to prevent and respond, in close coordination with the protection sector.

  • Provide all visibly pregnant women with clean delivery packages when access to skilled health providers and healthcare facilities cannot be guaranteed
  • Make a range of contraceptive methods available at healthcare facilities based on demand, in a private and confidential setting
    • Long-acting reversible and short-acting methods
    • Provide counselling that emphasises informed choice and effectiveness
  • Reduce the risk of sexual violence, ensure referrals and provide holistic support to survivors
    • Coordinate with other sectors to strengthen prevention and response
  • Inform the community of available services and the importance of seeking immediate medical care following sexual violence
  • Make clinical care and referral to other supportive services available for survivors of sexual violence

Essential Healthcare: Mental Health

Mental health and psychosocial problems are common among adults, adolescents and children in all humanitarian settings.

The extreme stressors associated with crises place people at increased risk of social, behavioural, psychological and psychiatric problems.

Key Interventions:

  • Mental health and psychosocial support involves multi-sectoral actions
  • Coordinate mental health and psychosocial supports across sectors
  • Work with community members, including marginalised people, to strengthen community self-help and social support
  • Orient staff and volunteers on how to offer psychological first aid
  • Make basic clinical mental healthcare available at every healthcare facility
Psychological First Aid

Psychological first aid needs to be available to people exposed to potentially traumatic events such as physical or sexual violence, witnessing atrocities and experiencing major injuries.

This is not a clinical intervention. It is a basic, humane and supportive response to suffering.

Example: MSF clinic in Beirut provided psychological first aid following the blast

Clinical Mental Healthcare

Brief all health staff and volunteers about available mental healthcare.

Train health providers according to evidence-based protocols.

Mental Health Gap (mhGAP) intervention guide provides evidence-based protocols

Common NCDs in Humanitarian Settings:

  • Diabetes
  • Cardiovascular disease (including hypertension, heart failure, strokes, chronic kidney disease)
  • Chronic lung disease (such as asthma and COPD)
  • Cancer

Behavioural Risk Factors:

Tobacco smoking and unhealthy diets contribute to NCD burden

Response Strategy:

Initial response should manage acute complications and avoid treatment interruption, followed by more comprehensive programmes.

Mental health and palliative care are specifically addressed as part of NCD care.

Case Study: Beirut Blast

August 4, 2020 at 6:08 PM

  • 218 killed
  • More than 6,000 injured
  • Half of Beirut city was destroyed
  • 50% of healthcare centers functional

Immediate Response

  • Formal organizations such as civil defense, Lebanese Red Cross, EMS responded immediately
  • Frantic search for survivors
  • Personal initiatives: Pamela the nurse and the three babies
  • The day after: hundreds-thousands took to the street for clean-up
  • Mobile clinics were deployed to offer services to the wounded

INGOs & NGOs Response

INGOs & NGOs provided:

  • Shelter, Food & Water
  • Mental health support
  • COVID-19 PPEs

MSF Medical Activities:

  • 265 injured cared for
  • 852 people suffering from non-communicable diseases
  • 50 benefited from consultations
  • 500 provided with COVID-19 tests

Summary

Key Takeaways:

  • Humanitarian crises arise from natural disasters, conflicts, and global injustice
  • The SDGs provide a framework for addressing humanitarian needs holistically
  • Health impacts occur across determinants, direct health outcomes, and healthcare systems
  • The HDP Nexus integrates humanitarian, development, and peacebuilding efforts
  • Multiple actors collaborate in humanitarian response, with coordination being crucial
  • Minimum standards ensure quality and equity in health service delivery
  • Essential healthcare covers trauma, communicable diseases, child health, SRH, mental health, and NCDs
  • Early recovery and long-term resilience are critical for sustainable outcomes
